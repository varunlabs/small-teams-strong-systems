## Chapter 6 — The Six Core Roles in an AI-Native Team

### Why Roles Matter More Than People

Teams often attribute performance to talent. When outcomes are strong, individuals are described as exceptional. When outcomes falter, the explanation turns to hiring gaps or capability mismatches. This framing assumes that people are the primary variable. It overlooks a quieter force: the structure within which people operate.

A role is not a title or a person. It is a structural position in a system of decisions. It defines what kinds of judgments are made, what tensions must be balanced, and where consequences accumulate. When roles are clear, choices align even under pressure. When roles are blurred, even capable individuals pull the system in conflicting directions.

This chapter maps the six roles of the **Six Forces Framework** — the complete operating model for AI-era teams introduced in Chapter 5. Each role is defined not by a job description but by the architectural force it applies. Remove any one force, and a predictable failure mode emerges. Keep all six in balance, and the team functions as a self-amplifying unit: a coherent cell that compounds effort rather than diffusing it.

Unclear roles generate friction long before visible failure appears. Decisions are revisited because authority is ambiguous. Responsibilities overlap without coordination. Critical tensions — between speed and stability, exploration and focus, autonomy and coherence — remain unresolved because no foundational position is accountable for holding them. The result is not incompetence, but drift.

Highly talented teams are particularly vulnerable to this drift. Skill can compensate temporarily for structural weakness. Strong individuals step into gaps, absorb ambiguity, and reconcile conflicts informally. Performance may appear resilient, yet it depends on constant correction. When pressure increases, these informal adjustments fail to scale.

Roles matter because they shape the flow of influence. Each role occupies a point where decisions affect downstream outcomes. If that point is undefined, influence disperses unpredictably. If it is well-defined, influence compounds. The distinction is architectural, not personal.

As leverage increases, the cost of unclear roles rises. Decisions travel further. Misalignment spreads faster. The stability of the system depends less on individual excellence and more on whether design-level positions are designed to absorb and balance competing demands.

*If performance depends on how responsibilities are structured rather than on who occupies them, what tensions must each role be designed to hold?*

---

### The Six Roles at a Glance

Before examining each role in detail, the complete framework is presented here. These six roles are the structural forces that keep a small, AI-amplified team coherent and compounding. Each role applies a distinct force. When any one is absent, a predictable failure mode emerges.

| # | Role | Force Applied | Failure Mode if Absent | Archetype |
|---|------|---------------|----------------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Direction and Coherence** | Aligns decisions toward long-term purpose; corrects drift | Activity without alignment; tradeoffs resolved inconsistently | The Compass |
| 2 | **Grounding in Reality** | Enforces contact with actual conditions; shortens feedback distance | Internal models grow confident but inaccurate; assumptions go untested | The Truth-Teller |
| 3 | **Technical Integrity** | Preserves structural correctness over time; maintains internal consistency | Hidden debt compounds; each addition introduces disproportionate friction | The Architect |
| 4 | **Leverage Design** | Redesigns where influence resides; determines what is worth amplifying | Effort is optimized locally but architecture remains unchanged; activity substitutes for effect | The Multiplier |
| 5 | **Learning and Feedback Acceleration** | Compresses the loop between decision and consequence; detects misalignment early | Errors embed themselves; assumptions harden beyond their validity | The Sensor |
| 6 | **External Connection** | Regulates the boundary between system and environment; curates outside signals | Isolation distorts judgment; internal models become self-reinforcing | The Bridge |

**Health Check — Observable Signals That a Force Is Weak:**

| Role | Signs of Weakness |
|------|-------------------|
| Direction and Coherence | Tradeoffs are resolved differently depending on who is in the room. Teams revisit the same strategic questions repeatedly without convergence. |
| Grounding in Reality | Surprises arrive late. Internal confidence is high but customer feedback contradicts it. Post-mortems reveal assumptions that were never tested. |
| Technical Integrity | Simple changes require unexpectedly large effort. New features introduce regressions in unrelated areas. Engineers avoid certain parts of the system. |
| Leverage Design | The team is busy but impact feels flat. The same manual work recurs without anyone redesigning it away. Automation exists but does not reduce decision load. |
| Learning and Feedback | The team ships frequently but rarely changes course. Retrospectives produce observations but not structural adjustments. Mistakes repeat across cycles. |
| External Connection | The product roadmap has not changed in response to external signals in the last quarter. Competitive moves or user behavior shifts are noticed late. |

These roles do not require six distinct individuals. In small configurations, one person may hold several. What matters is not who fills them, but that every force is actively present. When a force goes unrepresented, the system does not compensate automatically — it distorts.

The sections that follow examine each role in depth: the force it applies, the failure mode it prevents, and the tensions it must hold.

### 6.1 Direction and Coherence

Every system requires a function that defines where it is heading and corrects when it drifts. This function is not symbolic. It is structural. Without it, calls accumulate without alignment, and activity substitutes for progress.

Direction is often confused with articulation. Documents are written, priorities are announced, objectives are declared. Direction exists only if decisions across the system consistently reflect a shared understanding of long-term purpose. It is not what is stated; it is what persists through tradeoffs.

Tradeoffs reveal whether direction is real. When speed conflicts with quality, or expansion conflicts with focus, the system must resolve tension in a repeatable way. If outcomes vary depending on who is present or which pressure is strongest, direction is weak. If tradeoffs converge despite changing circumstances, direction is embedded.

Coherence operates as an ongoing corrective force. It monitors divergence and reconciles inconsistencies before they compound. As new information emerges and constraints shift, coherence reinterprets direction without abandoning it. Without coherence, even well-defined intent fragments. Different parts of the system optimize locally. Assumptions diverge. Terminology drifts.

In leveraged environments, the cost of weak direction increases. Decisions propagate further and faster. Small inconsistencies multiply. Direction and coherence therefore anchor every other role.

### 6.2 Grounding in Reality

If direction maintains orientation, grounding ensures that orientation corresponds to actual conditions. It is the function that prevents abstraction from detaching from evidence. Without grounding, systems become internally coherent yet externally misaligned.

Grounding is not sentiment or intuition. It is built-in exposure to constraint. It ensures that assumptions encounter resistance early, before they propagate widely. When grounding is weak, feedback arrives late or in distorted form. Signals are filtered through layers of interpretation, softened by hierarchy, or aggregated until their meaning is diluted.

Systems fail gradually under delayed feedback. Early inconsistencies appear small and manageable. Because they are not confronted directly, they compound. By the time failure becomes visible, it is embedded across multiple domains.

Filtered feedback produces a different pathology. Information moves upward selectively, emphasizing coherence over contradiction. Over time, internal models grow increasingly confident while becoming less accurate. The system mistakes agreement for validation.

Grounding interrupts this pattern by shortening the distance between decision and consequence. It is not a periodic review or corrective intervention. It is an embedded mechanism that enforces contact with reality as work unfolds. When direction defines long-term intent, grounding tests whether current movement aligns with actual constraints.

In high-leverage systems, grounding becomes more critical. Amplified judgments propagate rapidly. Without truth-enforcing mechanisms, misalignment spreads faster than it can be detected.

### 6.3 Technical Integrity

Direction establishes where the system intends to move. Grounding ensures that movement corresponds to reality. Technical integrity governs whether the system can continue moving without degrading itself. It is the function responsible for structural correctness over time.

Every system accumulates complexity. New capabilities are layered onto existing ones. Dependencies multiply. Shortcuts are introduced under pressure. Hidden technical debt is often framed as an engineering issue. In practice, it is a design failure. When integrity is not structurally owned, inconsistencies compound quietly. Decisions optimized for immediate delivery erode long-term coherence.

Technical integrity does not mean perfection. It means preserving internal consistency so that leverage compounds rather than fragments. When interfaces remain stable and assumptions remain explicit, new work builds on prior work without introducing disproportionate friction. When integrity weakens, each addition requires compensatory correction.

Time is the variable that exposes integrity. In the short term, degraded structure can appear efficient. Delivery accelerates because constraints are relaxed. In the long term, the cost reappears as rigidity, instability, or unexpected interactions.

As leverage increases, so does the cost of structural decay. Amplified systems magnify hidden weaknesses. Integrity therefore anchors the system across time.

### 6.4 The Leverage Designer

Leverage is often mistaken for efficiency. When output increases, the immediate assumption is that work has become faster or cheaper. This interpretation reduces leverage to a byproduct of optimization. It overlooks the architectural distinction between doing the same work with less effort and redesigning the system so that effort compounds.

Efficiency improves the performance of existing pathways. Leverage alters the pathways themselves. It changes where decisions are made, how they propagate, and how often they must be repeated. A leveraged system does not merely reduce friction within a process; it reduces the need for the process to be repeated at all.

Automation is frequently conflated with leverage. Automation executes predefined actions reliably and at scale. Leverage determines which actions are worth amplifying in the first place. Without foundational clarity about where influence should reside, automation multiplies activity rather than effect. Designing for leverage therefore precedes any decision about mechanization.

Designing for leverage begins with identifying points of influence. Not every task warrants amplification. Some actions are inherently contextual and should remain bounded. Others, if clarified once, can shape many outcomes consistently. The role responsible for leverage distinguishes between these categories and designs accordingly.

Leverage also requires coherence with other roles. Direction determines what should be amplified. Grounding tests whether amplification reflects reality. Technical integrity ensures that amplification does not erode structure over time.

*If leverage depends on redesigning where influence resides, what upstream decisions, once clarified, would eliminate the need for repeated downstream correction?*

### 6.5 Learning and Feedback Acceleration

Leverage increases the reach of choices. Learning determines whether that reach remains correct. Without accelerated feedback, amplified actions persist longer than their validity. The role responsible for learning therefore governs how quickly the system can detect misalignment and adjust.

Feedback is not simply information; it is information returned in time to influence subsequent decisions. When the distance between action and insight is long, errors embed themselves. Assumptions harden. Effort accumulates around interpretations that may no longer correspond to conditions. By the time correction arrives, the cost of reversal has multiplied.

Small teams are disproportionately exposed to slow feedback. They operate with limited slack. A prolonged misinterpretation can consume a significant share of available capacity. Their survival depends on compressing the loop between decision and consequence.

Learning acceleration is therefore design-level, not procedural. It depends on where signals enter the system, how directly they reach decision points, and whether they are interpreted without distortion.

Acceleration does not imply haste. It implies reduced latency between cause and effect. When feedback is timely, adjustments are smaller and less disruptive. When feedback is slow, correction requires abrupt change, increasing instability.

### 6.6 The External Connector

Every system exists within an environment it does not control. Markets shift, constraints evolve, and external actors respond in ways that cannot be fully predicted. The function responsible for external interfaces ensures that the boundary between system and world remains permeable enough to detect change, yet selective enough to preserve coherence.

Isolation distorts judgment gradually. When a system interacts primarily with its own outputs and internal interpretations, its models grow increasingly self-reinforcing. Decisions begin to optimize for internal consistency rather than external validity. Over time, divergence becomes difficult to perceive because reference points are circular.

External interfaces interrupt this cycle. They expose assumptions to conditions that do not accommodate internal logic. Friction at the boundary is not a nuisance; it is evidence.

However, external signals cannot be absorbed indiscriminately. The environment generates more information than any system can process without fragmentation. If every signal is treated as equally relevant, coherence erodes. Decision authority becomes reactive rather than intentional. Noise overwhelms structure.

As leverage increases, boundary integrity becomes more consequential. Amplified decisions interact with external conditions at a greater scale. Misalignment at the interface propagates rapidly.

### 6.7 The System as a Whole

Each role described in this chapter operates on a different axis of stability. Direction maintains orientation. Grounding enforces contact with reality. Technical integrity preserves structure over time. The leverage designer redesigns influence. Learning accelerates correction. External interfaces regulate boundary conditions. None of these functions exists in isolation. They overlap, reinforce, and at times constrain one another.

Tension between them is not a flaw; it is built-in necessity. Direction can drift into abstraction without grounding. Grounding can overwhelm coherence without curated boundaries. Leverage can destabilize integrity if amplification outpaces structural soundness. Learning can generate volatility if not anchored to intent. Stability emerges not from eliminating these tensions, but from sustaining them productively.

Importantly, these roles do not require distinct individuals to exist. In small configurations, one person may embody several simultaneously. What matters is not embodiment but presence. When a function is unrepresented, the system does not compensate automatically. It distorts.

Failure rarely arises because a role was never defined. More often, it emerges when a role collapses into another. Direction absorbs grounding and becomes insulated. Leverage overrides integrity and accumulates hidden fragility. Learning operates without coherence and produces oscillation.

The architecture described here is not an organizational chart. It is a set of interacting constraints. Their interplay determines whether leverage compounds or fragments over time. The system holds only if each tension remains active.


---

### 6.8 The Question This Chapter Leaves You With

Every system carries all of these roles, whether named or not. Direction exists in the tradeoffs that persist. Grounding exists in the signals that are heard — or ignored. Integrity reveals itself over time. Leverage shapes what compounds. Learning determines how quickly drift is corrected. External interfaces define what enters and what is kept out.

The discomfort arises when a function is present but unowned. Decisions are made, yet no one holds the tension they create. Signals arrive, yet no one is accountable for interpreting them. Structural decay accumulates, yet it is treated as incidental. The system continues to operate, but its balance depends on informal correction.

The question is not whether the roles exist. It is whether each one has a clear boundary of responsibility. When tension increases, which function collapses first? Where does ambiguity concentrate? What remains assumed rather than defined?

*If judgment ultimately resides within boundaries, where in your system are those boundaries unclear?*

---

**In Practice: Spotify's Squad Model and the Six Forces**

Spotify's renowned "squad" structure — introduced around 2012 — functioned as an early, intuitive approximation of the Six Forces Framework. Each squad (~six to eight people) was self-sufficient: a product owner held direction, an engineer maintained technical integrity, designers grounded choices in user evidence, and data roles accelerated feedback loops. Squads owned full delivery of their domain. Spotify called this "autonomy with alignment." It worked because each squad contained most of the structural forces. When squads later grew or merged without preserving role clarity, the model lost its compounding quality. The size hadn't changed the outcome. The role architecture had.

::: {.takeaways}
**Key Takeaways**

- Six functions are necessary for a team to operate coherently: direction, reality-grounding, technical integrity, leverage design, feedback acceleration, and external boundary management.
- No role is purely execution. Each requires active judgment within a bounded domain. When a role becomes only task-completion, it is no longer filling its structural function.
- Roles work as a system: a gap in any one function degrades the whole. The weakest boundary sets the coherence ceiling.
- Clear role boundaries reduce decision latency and prevent accountability from becoming diffuse. Ambiguous ownership is a design problem, not a personnel problem.
- AI amplifies the quality differential between strong and weak role-holders. The consequence of a judgment gap scales with the leverage available to that role.
:::

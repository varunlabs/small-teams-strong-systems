## 4. Reviewer Feedback

### 4.1 Figures and Diagrams
**Issue:**
A number of figures — particularly those supporting the book's core arguments around coordination cost, leverage, scaling, feedback loops, and organizational complexity — reinforce ideas already explained in the text rather than adding new insight.

**Why it matters:**
In several places, a reader can skip the figure without losing understanding. The strongest diagrams should help readers see relationships, patterns, and trade-offs that are difficult to communicate through prose alone.

**Recommended Improvement:**
- Review key argument figures rather than all figures
- Strengthen diagrams that explain: coordination cost, leverage, scaling behaviour, feedback loops, organizational complexity
- Use curves and visual relationships where appropriate
- Ensure captions communicate the insight, not just the title
- Maintain a consistent visual language throughout the book

**Overall:** The concepts behind the figures are sound. The opportunity is to make the most important diagrams as strong as the surrounding writing.

---

### 4.2 WhatsApp Example (Chapter 4 / Chapter 5)
**Issue:** The manuscript states that WhatsApp had approximately 55 engineers serving 450 million users at acquisition.

**Research Finding:** Available reporting consistently supports approximately 35 engineers at acquisition (with around 50 total employees overall).

**Why it matters:** This is a specific factual claim. If a reader verifies the number and finds a different figure, it weakens trust in the example.

**Recommended Improvement:** Update the engineer count to approximately 35 engineers or provide a source supporting the current figure.

**Status:** Open

---

### 4.3 Netflix Example (Chapter 10)
**Issue:** The chapter presents "Informed Captain" as a Netflix decision-making model.

**Research Finding:** The term could not be verified as an official Netflix framework. Public Netflix material more commonly references "Freedom and Responsibility" and "Context, Not Control."

**Why it matters:** The concern is not the idea itself. The concern is attributing the idea directly to Netflix when public evidence for that terminology could not be found.

**Recommended Improvement:** Either present "Informed Captain" as the author's concept, or replace it with documented Netflix terminology.

**Status:** Open

---

### 4.4 OpenAI Example (Chapter 12)
**Issue:** The chapter may give readers the impression that OpenAI is publicly known to operate through small, high-leverage teams in the way described by the book.

**Research Finding:** Publicly documented teams exist (Superalignment, Preparedness, Safety Systems), but there is insufficient public evidence to support broader claims about OpenAI's overall organizational structure.

**Why it matters:** The example moves from documented public information into assumptions about internal operating design.

**Recommended Improvement:** Reference specific publicly documented teams rather than drawing conclusions about OpenAI's broader organizational model.

**Status:** Open

---

### 4.5 Waymo Example (Chapter 12)
**Issue:** The chapter may give readers the impression that Waymo is evidence of a documented small-cell organizational model.

**Research Finding:** Public information supports Waymo beginning with a relatively small technical team but does not strongly support claims about its current internal operating structure.

**Why it matters:** The example uses a real company to support a structural argument without sufficient public evidence for the specific organizational claim.

**Recommended Improvement:** Position Waymo as an example of a small-team origin story rather than evidence of a verified organizational design model.

**Status:** Open
